# Literature Review: public service motivation

## Summary

Public service motivation (PSM) has been extensively studied over the past two decades, evolving from foundational theoretical constructs to more nuanced empirical investigations. Kim et al. (2012) contributed a comprehensive international approach by developing and validating a revised 16-item, four-dimensional PSM measurement instrument across 12 countries. Their findings highlight considerable variability in the meaning and measurement of PSM across cultural and linguistic contexts, questioning the feasibility of a universal PSM scale. Building on such foundational work, Ritz, Brewer, and Neumann (2016) synthesized 323 publications through a systematic literature review, mapping the rapid growth and thematic diversification in PSM research. Their review uncovers prevailing research designs and methodological approaches, common empirical findings, and practical implications, while also identifying strengths and limitations within the existing scholarship.

## Themes

Two core themes emerge from the literature. First, measurement and conceptual clarity remain central, as evidenced by Kim et al.’s (2012) efforts to refine PSM metrics internationally, suggesting that PSM’s dimensionality and interpretation can vary significantly across populations. This underscores the importance of cultural sensitivity in PSM research. Second, the body of empirical research on PSM is increasingly robust and diverse, encompassing a multitude of research designs and analytical strategies (Ritz et al., 2016). Their systematic review reveals consistent interest in PSM’s antecedents, outcomes, and organizational implications, illustrating both the theoretical richness and applied relevance of the concept. Moreover, Ritz et al. (2016) emphasize the growing impact and dissemination of PSM research in academic literature, as well as its practical contributions to public administration and management.

## Gaps and Next Steps

Despite advances in measuring and understanding PSM, significant gaps persist. Kim et al. (2012) draw attention to the challenge of measurement invariance, indicating that cross-cultural differences complicate direct comparisons and the development of a universally applicable instrument. This calls for further research focusing on culturally adaptive frameworks and localized validation. Additionally, while Ritz et al. (2016) provide a broad synthesis of empirical studies, they highlight the need for more integrative and theory-driven research designs, including longitudinal and experimental approaches, to establish stronger causal inferences. Future studies should also explore emerging contextual factors influencing PSM, such as globalization and shifting public sector environments, to enrich the conceptual depth and practical utility of PSM theory.
